1861
April 12, 1861
The Fall of Fort Sumter
Confederate artillery under General P.G.T. Beauregard bombarded the Union-held Fort Sumter in Charleston Harbor, South Carolina, for 34 hours. The fort's commander, Major Robert Anderson, surrendered on April 14. No soldiers died in combat.
The first shots of the war. Lincoln's call for 75,000 volunteers to suppress the rebellion immediately followed.
July 21, 1861
First Battle of Bull Run
Near Manassas, Virginia, Union forces under General Irvin McDowell met Confederate troops commanded by Beauregard and Joseph Johnston. After initial Union advances, a Confederate counterattack, anchored by General Thomas Jackson's brigade, shattered federal lines. Civilians who had come to watch fled alongside the retreating soldiers.
Shattered Northern illusions of a quick, easy war. Forced both sides to prepare for a prolonged, massive conflict.
March 8-9, 1862
Battle of Hampton Roads: Monitor vs. Virginia
The Confederate ironclad CSS Virginia devastated Union wooden warships on March 8, sinking two vessels. The next day, the Union's own ironclad USS Monitor arrived and the two ships battled to a draw in the first engagement between iron-hulled warships in history.
Made wooden warships obsolete overnight and launched a new era of naval warfare worldwide.
1862
April 6-7, 1862
Battle of Shiloh
Confederate General Albert Sidney Johnston launched a surprise dawn assault on Ulysses S. Grant's army camped at Pittsburg Landing, Tennessee. Johnston was killed on the first day. Union reinforcements turned the tide, and Grant drove the Confederates back on April 7. More than 23,000 casualties fell in two days.
The staggering bloodshed ended any hope of a short war and demonstrated that the South could not be defeated by a single decisive blow.
September 17, 1862
Battle of Antietam
General Robert E. Lee's first invasion of the North was stopped by General George McClellan along Antietam Creek in Maryland. With about 22,720 total casualties, it remains the bloodiest single day in American military history. Lee withdrew to Virginia the following day.
The Union's narrow victory gave Lincoln the opening he needed to issue the Emancipation Proclamation and helped keep Britain from recognizing the Confederacy.
January 1, 1863
The Emancipation Proclamation
President Abraham Lincoln issued an executive order declaring that all enslaved persons in Confederate-held states "shall be then, thenceforward, and forever free." While it did not immediately free everyone, it fundamentally changed the character of the war and authorized the enlistment of Black men into the Union Army.
Redefined the war as a moral crusade against slavery, galvanized Black Americans, and made it much harder for European powers to aid the Confederacy.
1863
April 30 - May 6, 1863
Battle of Chancellorsville
Despite being badly outnumbered by General Joseph Hooker's Army of the Potomac, Lee and Stonewall Jackson executed a daring flanking maneuver that routed Union forces near Fredericksburg, Virginia. Jackson was accidentally shot by his own men and died days later.
Lee's greatest tactical victory, but the loss of Stonewall Jackson was a major blow from which the Confederacy never fully recovered.
July 1-3, 1863
Battle of Gettysburg
Lee's second invasion of the North ended in catastrophe in southern Pennsylvania. Three days of brutal fighting against General George Meade's Army of the Potomac culminated in Pickett's Charge, a disastrous assault on July 3. The battle produced roughly 51,000 total casualties.
The war's turning point. Lee never again mounted a major offensive, and the Confederacy was permanently forced into a defensive posture.
July 4, 1863
Surrender of Vicksburg
After a 47-day siege by Ulysses S. Grant's forces, Confederate General John C. Pemberton surrendered the fortress city of Vicksburg, Mississippi, along with nearly 30,000 soldiers. The capture on Independence Day was met with enormous celebration in the North.
Gave the Union complete control of the Mississippi River, splitting the Confederacy in two and cutting off vital supply lines from Texas and Louisiana.
1864
May 4 - June 24, 1864
Grant's Overland Campaign
Newly appointed General-in-Chief Ulysses S. Grant launched a relentless offensive against Lee's Army of Northern Virginia through the Wilderness, Spotsylvania Court House, and Cold Harbor. Unlike earlier Union commanders, Grant refused to retreat after each costly battle, pressing south toward Richmond.
Introduced the strategy of attritional war. The Union could absorb the losses. The Confederacy could not.
November 15 - December 21, 1864
Sherman's March to the Sea
After capturing Atlanta, General William T. Sherman led 60,000 Union soldiers on a 300-mile march from Atlanta to Savannah, Georgia. His army cut a broad path of destruction through the Confederate heartland, targeting railroads, supply depots, and infrastructure.
Devastated the Confederacy's economy, weakened civilian morale, and accelerated the war's end.
1865
April 9, 1865
Lee's Surrender at Appomattox
Surrounded and exhausted after the fall of Petersburg, General Robert E. Lee surrendered the Army of Northern Virginia to General Ulysses S. Grant at the McLean House in Appomattox Court House, Virginia. Grant offered generous terms that allowed many Confederate soldiers to return home with dignity.
Effectively ended the Civil War and helped begin the long process of national reconciliation.
April 14-15, 1865
Assassination of President Lincoln
Just days after Lee's surrender, Confederate sympathizer John Wilkes Booth shot President Abraham Lincoln at Ford's Theatre in Washington, D.C. during a performance of Our American Cousin. Lincoln died the following morning. He was the first U.S. president to be assassinated.
Removed the one leader most capable of guiding a careful Reconstruction and changed the course of postwar America.
